# Brand Foundation — Anagha Todalbagi

The website is one artifact. The brand is the sentence people repeat about her when she's not in the room. Everything below is built to make that sentence accurate, memorable, and easy to verify.

## 1. Positioning

**For** founders and executives of Bay Area deep-tech companies (and the recruiters who serve them)
**who need** product leadership that engineering teams respect and customers feel,
**Anagha Todalbagi** is a product leader with eight years of backend engineering underneath —
**who** takes hard technical products from preview to GA and can still open the codebase.
**Unlike** career PMs who narrate the roadmap, her claims resolve to public artifacts: GA announcements, recorded talks, shipped features.

## 2. The brand idea: the arrow

Her entire story is transitions, honestly earned:

    backend engineer → product leader
    seed (5th hire) → Series C → Series D → big tech
    private preview → public preview → GA → GA+
    stream → insight (her actual product domain)

**The arrow (→) is the brand device.** It is native to her audience's vocabulary (every deep-tech exec reads "preview → GA" without translation), it is evidence-shaped (an arrow states a completed transition, not an adjective), and it is typographic — no drawn logo, no mascot, nothing to age badly.

**Monogram:** `a→t` — her initials, joined by the device. Lowercase, set in the brand type, used small: favicon, OG card corner, talk-slide footer, resume header. Never large, never decorated.

**Tagline:** **"Product leader who still ships."** Audited hard: "ships" is the most-claimed verb in product — but "still" carries the eight engineering years and cannot be claimed by a career PM. It survives on the condition that evidence sits within one glance of it, always.

## 3. Verbal identity

- **Evidence over assertion** (the existing rule, now brand law). Every sentence resolves to a link, number, date, or named program.
- **Write in arrows.** "Private preview → GA+." "5th engineering hire → GA launches at Microsoft." The device is also the sentence structure.
- Short declaratives, sentence case, no exclamation marks. Banned: proven, passionate, innovative, visionary, results-driven, "player-coach" (it's the conclusion visitors should draw; saying it ourselves violates the law).
- Numbers stated plainly: 8 years, 5th hire, 9 years on stage, 15,000+ applicants.

## 4. Visual identity

- **Surfaces:** warm paper `#EBEAE5` and ink `#161613` — two-surface monochrome system. Light surface for reading (site body, resume); ink surface for statement moments (OG card, LinkedIn banner, talk title slides, site footer). No accent color: recognition comes from the device + surface consistency, not a hue. Color enters only through live content (video playback).
- **Type:** Inter variable, weights 400/500 only. The wordmark is simply the name set at 500, −0.03em. The system IS typography; owning a typeface habit is the identity.
- **Imagery:** only real artifacts — her stage stills (grayscale), product proof links, named programs. No stock, no illustration, no memoji.
- **Motion signature (one):** converging streamlines — many faint ink paths gathering into a single line that resolves left → right. It is the arrow, animated; also literally her product domain (streams → insight). Ink at 4–7% opacity. Site hero only; a static frame of it is the OG/banner background texture.

## 5. Dual-mode consumption (text people and visual people)

Same content, two spines, never two versions:

| | Text consumer (recruiter, exec scanning on phone) | Visual consumer (founder, designer-brained exec) |
|---|---|---|
| Hook | Name + tagline + one evidence line | Streamline motion resolving behind the name |
| Proof | Ledger rows ending in links; dates in tabular figures | Grayscale stage stills; glyph-free, image-real |
| Depth | Semantic HTML, copy-pastable, JSON-LD with exact dates, print stylesheet = one-page resume | Click-to-play talks (color returns on play) |

The page must read perfectly with images and motion stripped (text mode), and land its story with all text blurred (visual mode). Both tests run before ship.

## 6. Brand kit (deliverables beyond the site)

1. Website (the flagship).
2. **OG card** — ink surface, static streamline frame, name + tagline + `a→t`. This is what executives see when her link lands in Slack/LinkedIn — it renders on every share.
3. **Favicon** — `a→t` on paper.
4. **LinkedIn banner** — same composition as OG card, sized 1584×396.
5. **Print stylesheet** — the site prints as a clean one-page resume (recruiters do this).
6. Talk-slide title template (paper + ink, streamline frame, `a→t` footer) — optional, post-launch.

## 7. Critique log (what was killed, and why)

- **Accent colors** (v2 orange, then a considered terminal-green) — killed. Orange read as clutter; phosphor green is "engineer cosplay." Her references are monochrome; discipline is the memorable move.
- **"Player-coach" as public copy** — killed from all surfaces. It's other people's word for her; using it ourselves is self-assessment, which the brand law forbids.
- **Company logos, product glyphs, stat count-ups, split panels** (v2) — killed. Devices multiplied faster than meaning. One device (→), one motion, one image class (stage stills).
- **Mist-as-weather hero** (v3) — refined, not killed. Aimless drift became convergence: the motion now states the brand idea instead of decorating it.
- **Monogram risk** reviewed — monograms on personal sites can read self-important. Verdict: keep, because it's lowercase, typographic, and functional (favicon/OG need a mark); constraint: never displayed large.
- **"Preview → GA, repeatedly" as tagline** — considered, killed: too Microsoft-internal for a headhunter. "Product leader who still ships." tests better against both audiences.
